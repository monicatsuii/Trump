Twitter was a major part of Trump’s presidency and his administration; with a massive social media following, he could shape the nation’s politics and influence media heavily. By posting multiple times daily, Trump’s tweets are constantly trending which reinforce his strong online influence, creating an advantage for his presidential campaigns (Kroon, 2018).

This study utilises an archive of all tweets from President Trump’s terms in the office to analyse trends and relationships in comparison with the US stock market.

*Average from second half of presidency

How often did Trump tweet?

President Trump heavily relied on Twitter as his main source of communication to the public. It can be seen in the graph, the volume that Trump has posted on the platform in comparison to events that are occuring, especially during the re-election period. Studies have shown that he has used Twitter to spread consipracy theories, fake information and extremist content ("How Trump Reshaped the Presidency in Over 11,000 Tweets", 2019). This is seen in Trump’s favourite phrase, “fake news” roughly 1,000 times during his presidency.

Trump's tweets and the S&P 500

Trump has mentioned the words “stock” 152 times throughout his history of tweets during his presidency, with an average sentiment score of 0.2. Being that his tweets lean towards a positive sentiment, Trump claims credit for rises in the stock market often when it would increase. In this graph, we analyse the relationship between Trump’s tweets related to “stocks” and the performance of the S&P 500 Index, which is a “market capitalization-weighted index of the 500 largest publicly-traded companies in the US” (Kenton, 2021).

US S&P 500 Sectors in 2020

As of the end of 2020, Global energy demand has drastically reduced due to the pandemic and financials were also negatively impacted due to high unemployment rate. Energy stocks declined drastically as well due to Trump’s trade war with China. On the other hand, information technology sector has risen despite enacted policies such as skilled immigration or even critisizing tech giant Amazon (Scheld, 2020).
